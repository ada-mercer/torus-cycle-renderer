# Natural Emergence of Incomplete Quark Cycles in M1

Status: derivation scaffold / interpretation-tightening note

This note explains why quark-like branches in the current M1 picture are naturally led toward **incomplete or defect-bearing cycles**, rather than fully self-contained isolated resonant cycles.

---

## 1) Objective

Show that the current quark interpretation is not merely a visualization convenience.

Target claim:

> In M1, quark-like sectors are naturally modeled as **incomplete closure sectors** on the common internal carrier, with full closure restored only through composite or weak-assisted structure.

This note does **not** claim a final first-principles derivation of the exact fractions yet.
It does aim to show that the mechanism itself is structurally natural.

---

## 2) Starting locks from M1

We keep the following framework locks fixed.

### L1. Common carrier geometry
Internal state lives on a shared two-cycle carrier:
\[
(\theta_f,\theta_b)\in T^2.
\]

So all sectors are to be described on one common internal geometry, not by giving each particle type its own topology.

### L2. Stable isolated states require closure
For stable asymptotic states, closure is required:
\[
\frac{\dot\theta_f}{\dot\theta_b}\in\mathbb Q
\]
and more generally the relevant phase/state variables must return consistently after a full orbit.

### L3. Quark-like sectors are not ordinary free asymptotic sectors
The internal-geometry formulation already introduces a quark-like closure-defect idea:
- isolated nonzero net defect is disallowed as a stable free state,
- composite combinations with net closure are allowed.

So quarks are already distinguished from lepton-like sectors by **closure status**, not by carrier replacement.

### L4. Weak channels are the natural transition/compensation slot
Weak-like channels are already the preferred place for:
- chirality-sensitive transitions,
- non-photon-like compensation structure,
- massive transition-sector dynamics.

So if a sector carries an unresolved closure mismatch, the weak channel is the most natural compensating candidate already present in the framework.

---

## 3) Why incomplete closure is the natural quark option

Suppose we insist on all four locks above.
Then quark-like sectors must satisfy two competing requirements:

1. they must live on the same carrier as the other sectors,
2. but they must not appear as ordinary isolated fully closed asymptotic states.

The cleanest way to satisfy both is:

- keep the same carrier,
- keep the same local mode language,
- but assign the quark sector a **nonzero closure defect**.

That is, after one nominal internal orbit, a quark-like state returns only up to a residual phase or residual closure mismatch:
\[
\Delta\varphi_q = 2\pi(N_q + \delta_q),
\qquad 0 < \delta_q < 1.
\]

This is the most economical difference between:
- a lepton-like closed resonant state,
- and a quark-like non-asymptotic state,

while preserving one shared geometry.

So the incomplete-cycle picture is not an arbitrary embellishment. It is the **minimal structural move** once one refuses both topology proliferation and free-quark asymptotic closure.

---

## 4) Why the defect should be fractional

On a compact periodic carrier, closure failure does not naturally appear as a random non-compact drift first.
It appears as a **residual fraction of a cycle**.

That is because a periodic geometry naturally quantizes mismatch relative to full return:
\[
\text{full return} \leftrightarrow 2\pi\times \text{integer},
\]
so incomplete return naturally takes the form
\[
2\pi\times \text{(integer + fraction)}.
\]

Hence a quark-like defect parameter
\[
\delta_q
\]
is not ad hoc; it is the natural bookkeeping variable for an orbit that is nearly or structurally periodic but not individually admissible as a free closed state.

---

## 5) Why thirds are the first natural candidate

A defect fraction must do two jobs:

1. prevent isolated closure,
2. allow low-order composite closure.

The smallest nontrivial denominator that naturally supports irreducible three-body completion is
\[
3.
\]

This makes
\[
\delta_q \in \left\{\frac13,\frac23\right\}
\]
a natural first hypothesis.

Reason:
- denominator 2 mainly supports pair completion,
- denominator 3 is the first denominator that naturally supports distinct three-part composite cancellation,
- it therefore fits baryon-like closure logic more naturally than denominator 2.

With this choice, global closure can occur through sums like
\[
\delta_1 + \delta_2 + \delta_3 \in \mathbb Z.
\]

So thirds are not yet uniquely derived, but they are the **minimal rich denominator** for a quark-like composite closure sector.

---

## 6) Composite closure principle

Let a composite contain several defect-bearing sectors.
Then the natural admissibility condition is:
\[
\sum_a \delta_a \in \mathbb Z.
\]

Interpretation:
- isolated quark-like sectors remain incomplete,
- composites become admissible when their total defect cancels.

This gives a simple M1-native picture of confinement-like behavior:
- free quark: closure defect unresolved,
- hadronic composite: closure restored globally.

That is more natural in M1 than assuming quarks are fully closed objects that simply fail to appear freely for unrelated reasons.

---

## 7) Why weak-assisted closure appears naturally

If a quark-like sector carries defect
\[
\delta_q,
\]
and a weak channel contributes a compensating cycle phase
\[
\eta_w,
\]
then the most natural low-order assisted-closure condition is
\[
m_q\,\delta_q - n_w\,\eta_w \in \mathbb Z.
\]

This is exactly the right structure if:
- quark cycles are incomplete on their own,
- weak channels are allowed to carry compensating internal phase transport.

So weak assistance is not a decorative add-on. It is the first compensation mechanism the existing framework already provides.

---

## 8) Low-order resonance expectation

Once defect-bearing quark cycles are admitted, one expects the cleanest observable locks to occur at **small integers**:
\[
m_q : n_w = \text{small integers}.
\]

That is because low-order rational locks dominate the readable near-closure structure on compact cycle spaces.

So relations of the form
\[
2T_q \approx T_W
\]
or similar low-order assisted resonances are exactly what one would expect to appear first if the quark sector is incomplete but weak-coupled.

This does not prove a specific ratio from first principles, but it explains why low-order weak-assisted locks are structurally favored.

---

## 9) Consequence for interpretation of renders

Under this picture, a quark render should be read as one of the following:
- a defect-bearing cycle representative,
- a resonant skeleton of a non-asymptotic sector,
- a clean probe mode of a sector whose full closure is only restored with assistance or composition.

It should **not** automatically be read as:
- a fully self-contained isolated quark state,
- a proof of exact asymptotic closure,
- a final QCD-equivalent solution.

This aligns the visualization policy with the theoretical role of the quark branch.

---

## 10) What is natural already, and what remains open

### Natural already
The following now look structurally well-motivated:
- quarks as incomplete closure sectors,
- fractional defect bookkeeping,
- composite cancellation as admissibility rule,
- weak-assisted compensation as first transition mechanism,
- thirds as the first nontrivial minimal denominator worth testing.

### Still open
The following remain to be derived more sharply:
- why the exact assignments should be `1/3` and `2/3`,
- why a specific weak compensation fraction should take its current value,
- why one low-order lock should dominate over nearby alternatives,
- how composite closure should be formalized for meson- and baryon-like branches beyond the current scaffold.

---

## 11) Working conclusion

The current M1 structure already strongly suggests:

> Quark-like branches are naturally modeled not as fully self-closing isolated resonant states, but as **defect-bearing cycles** whose admissibility is restored only through composite or weak-assisted closure.

This is a structural consequence of:
- one shared carrier geometry,
- closure as the criterion for asymptotic stability,
- refusal of free isolated quark closure,
- and the presence of weak transition channels.

So the incomplete-cycle picture should be treated as a natural M1 mechanism, not merely a rendering patch.
